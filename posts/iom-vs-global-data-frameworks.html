IoM data governance versus the EU, UK, and Singapore: what makes the difference

Registries.im

Every major jurisdiction now has a data governance framework. The EU has three. The UK updated its framework in 2025. Singapore has been refining its approach since 2013.

What none of them have is a statutory register of data assets – a mechanism for treating datasets as property, registering ownership, and using governed data as a financial instrument.

That is what makes the Isle of Man’s approach different.

What other frameworks actually do

European Union: compliance, not property

The EU has layered three instruments since 2018.

GDPR (applicable since 2018) establishes personal data rights and obligations. It is a compliance and protection framework. It creates obligations on organisations that hold personal data; it does not create property rights in data.

Data Governance Act (applicable since September 2023) enables cross-sector data sharing. It creates a category of registered “data intermediaries” – neutral facilitators of B2B and B2C data sharing – and establishes “data altruism organisations” that collect data for public interest purposes. It requires member states to create single points of contact for data sharing requests. It does not create a register of data assets, does not confer property rights in datasets, and does not provide a mechanism for using data as loan collateral.

Data Act (force 11 January 2024, applicable from 12 September 2025) addresses who can access and use data generated by connected devices. It creates mandatory sharing obligations on device manufacturers and data holders, transparency requirements in data processing contracts, and rules against vendor lock-in in cloud services. It is a data access and portability framework, not a data asset property framework.

The EU’s three instruments together constitute an extensive compliance and data sharing regime. They impose significant obligations on organisations that handle data. They do not create a mechanism for registering datasets as property, placing data on a balance sheet, or using a governed dataset as collateral for a loan.

United Kingdom: smart data, not asset registration

The UK Data (Use and Access) Act received Royal Assent on 19 June 2025.

Its key provisions include Smart Data schemes – a framework enabling the Secretary of State to require data holders to share customer data with authorised third parties, modelled on Open Banking. It also creates a National Underground Asset Register, a registry for the locations of underground infrastructure, and reforms to the Information Commissioner’s Office.

Smart Data is significant for sectors where data portability creates consumer value. It is not a data asset registration system. The National Underground Asset Register is a registry for physical infrastructure locations, not for datasets as assets. No provision of the UK DUA Act creates a mechanism for registering data as property, valuing data as a balance sheet asset, or using data as financial collateral.

Singapore: data sharing hub, not data asset jurisdiction

Singapore’s Personal Data Protection Act (2013, significantly updated in 2021) is administered by the Personal Data Protection Commission. Singapore has invested heavily in AI governance frameworks, sector-specific data sharing initiatives, and cross-border data flow agreements.

Its 2024 to 2026 developments include PDPC advisory guidelines on AI systems, the IMDA Model AI Governance Framework for Generative AI (2024) and Agentic AI (January 2026), and Monetary Authority of Singapore data governance guidelines for financial institutions.

Singapore positions itself as a data-sharing hub and AI governance leader. Its frameworks govern responsible data handling and AI deployment. No framework found in Singapore creates a statutory mechanism for registering datasets as property, using data as formal balance-sheet assets, or treating data as loan collateral.

Jersey and Guernsey: adequacy without asset infrastructure

Jersey and Guernsey both hold EU adequacy status, reaffirmed in January 2024 as part of the same European Commission review that confirmed the Isle of Man’s adequacy. Both are well-established financial jurisdictions with mature legal systems.

Neither has enacted data asset legislation. Neither has a data asset register. Neither has a statutory mechanism for treating datasets as property assets. Their data protection frameworks – the Data Protection (Jersey) Law 2018 and the Data Protection (Bailiwick of Guernsey) Law 2017 – are GDPR-aligned compliance frameworks.

What the Isle of Man has done differently

The Isle of Man’s Foundations (Amendment) Bill, passed by Tynwald, creates two instruments that no other jurisdiction has:

The Data Asset Foundation (DAF): a legal wrapper for a specific, curated, governed dataset. A DAF gives data a formal legal identity – it can be listed on a balance sheet, used as collateral, licensed, transferred, or factored into a valuation. The same legal mechanisms that allow a company to hold and transfer property apply to a DAF-registered dataset.

The Data Asset Register (DAR): a statutory register for data assets, the first of its kind in the world. It functions analogously to the Isle of Man’s ship registry or land registry – an authoritative, governed record of what data assets exist, who holds them, what their provenance is, and what rights attach to them.

The critical distinction from every other framework: the IoM approach creates property rights in data, not just obligations around it.

The institutional combination

The Isle of Man brings three elements that no other jurisdiction combines.

Established registry competence: the Isle of Man Ship Registry is consistently ranked among the world’s leading quality registries by the Paris Memorandum of Understanding. The Isle of Man Aircraft Registry is one of the largest in Europe. The Isle of Man Companies Registry underpins one of the world’s most respected offshore financial jurisdictions. The institutional capability to operate a trusted, internationally recognised register already exists.

EU adequacy: reaffirmed by the European Commission in January 2024, the IoM’s EU adequacy decision means that personal data registered on the Isle of Man carries the same legal standing for EU-origin data transfers as data registered within the EU itself. This is not available in most jurisdictions – only eleven jurisdictions worldwide hold EU adequacy status.

UNESCO Biosphere designation: the Isle of Man is the world’s first whole-jurisdiction UNESCO Biosphere Reserve. This is relevant for data governance because it creates an institutional framework for whole-island environmental monitoring – the kind of location-specific, ecosystem-condition data that TNFD nature-related disclosures require. A company seeking to comply with TNFD in the Isle of Man can draw on a governed, provenanced, institutionally-backed environmental data infrastructure that exists nowhere else.

No other jurisdiction in the world combines all three: statutory data asset registration, EU adequacy, and whole-jurisdiction environmental data governance.

What this means for companies and data holders

For a company that holds valuable data and wants to capitalise it – use it as collateral, license it commercially, include it in an M&A valuation – the governance framework available in the jurisdiction matters.

Registering a data asset under a framework that creates no property rights, confers no legal separability, and provides no authoritative record of ownership provides no material advantage over doing nothing. The data remains, legally, what it was before: an operational asset with no formal status.

Registering a data asset under a framework that creates statutory property rights, provides an authoritative governance record, and is backed by a jurisdiction with EU adequacy and a half-century track record of registry administration is a different proposition entirely.
