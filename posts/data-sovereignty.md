# Data sovereignty: why companies are rethinking where their data is governed

Five years ago, data sovereignty was a concern for governments and intelligence services. Today it is a procurement question, a due diligence consideration, and increasingly a regulatory requirement.

Organisations are asking — and being asked — where their data is governed. The answers have commercial and legal consequences that few have fully mapped.

## What data sovereignty means

Data sovereignty refers to the principle that data is subject to the laws and governance structures of the jurisdiction in which it is collected, processed, or stored.

The practical implications:

- Data collected from EU citizens is subject to EU GDPR, regardless of where the controller is based
- Data stored in a US-based data centre may be accessible to US authorities under instruments like the CLOUD Act, regardless of the nationality of the data subject
- Data governed under a jurisdiction with weak enforcement may be less valuable to buyers who need governance assurance
- Data governed under a jurisdiction with EU adequacy status can be transferred to EU entities without additional legal mechanisms

Sovereignty is not just about where data physically sits — it is about which legal framework governs it, which authority enforces that framework, and what rights and obligations flow from that jurisdiction's laws.

## Why it has become a boardroom issue

Several converging factors have pushed data sovereignty onto executive agendas:

**Regulatory fragmentation.** The EU, UK, US, China, and many other jurisdictions have enacted or are developing data governance frameworks that create conflicting obligations. An organisation operating across multiple jurisdictions needs to understand which framework governs which data — and choose that framework deliberately, not by default.

**Geopolitical risk.** Organisations have become aware that infrastructure and platform dependencies create exposure. If your data is governed by a platform headquartered in a jurisdiction subject to rapidly changing policy, the legal basis on which you hold and process that data can shift without your control.

**Counterparty due diligence.** Buyers of data assets, investors in data-driven businesses, and partners in data sharing arrangements are now asking governance questions that include jurisdiction. Where is this data governed? Under which framework? By which supervisory authority? A clear, favourable answer to these questions accelerates transactions. An unclear answer, or one involving a jurisdiction with weak enforcement or geopolitical risk, slows or kills them.

**Supply chain obligations.** The EU Data Act and related frameworks are introducing obligations on organisations to know and disclose their data supply chains. Ignorance of where data is governed is no longer a defensible position.

## What makes a jurisdiction suitable for data governance

Not all jurisdictions are equal as data governance anchors. The characteristics that matter:

**Legal stability.** The framework governing data rights must be durable and predictable. Jurisdictions with frequent, unpredictable legislative change create uncertainty in long-term data agreements.

**Independent enforcement.** A supervisory authority that is genuinely independent of government and commercial interests provides assurance that governance obligations are enforced impartially. This is a legal requirement under GDPR-family frameworks and a commercial signal to counterparties.

**International recognition.** A jurisdiction whose data governance framework is formally recognised by major trading partners — through adequacy decisions, bilateral agreements, or treaty frameworks — eliminates legal friction in cross-border data transactions.

**Track record.** Jurisdictions that already operate trusted, internationally recognised registries for other asset classes have demonstrated the administrative competence and regulatory culture required for data asset registration.

**Absence of conflicting obligations.** A jurisdiction that is not subject to extra-territorial data access laws from powerful third-country governments provides a cleaner governance environment than one that is.

## The Isle of Man position

The Isle of Man is not a member of the EU and is not subject to the CLOUD Act (a US instrument). It has its own Information Commissioner as an independent supervisory authority. It holds EU adequacy status. It has decades of experience operating trusted specialist registries. Its legal system is founded on English common law with an independent judiciary.

For organisations seeking a jurisdiction for data asset governance that combines EU recognition, genuine independence from major-power extra-territorial reach, and demonstrated registry competence, the Isle of Man is one of a small number of viable options — and the one with the most developed specialist registry infrastructure.
